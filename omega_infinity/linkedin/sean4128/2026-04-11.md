Biomedical reasoning becomes difficult when evidence appears coherent in one part of the stack and unresolved in another.

A translational gap is one example of that difficulty. Mechanistic structure may support causal plausibility, genetic structure may reinforce target relevance, and clinical structure may still fail to close the loop at the human outcome layer. This does not create a simple absence of support. It creates a structural decision problem in which the central uncertainty remains exactly where transfer depends on it.

Cross-layer uncertainty matters because mechanistic, genetic, and clinical layers do different work. Mechanistic evidence supports plausibility. Genetic evidence can strengthen target-level alignment. Clinical evidence determines whether the proposed effect resolves in human outcomes. When one layer remains open, confidence does not propagate cleanly through the stack.

That is why structural decision difficulty persists even when evidence looks directionally encouraging. A stack can show coherence below the clinical layer and still remain non-advancing. The challenge is not merely missing information. It is determining whether the remaining uncertainty is peripheral or whether it sits directly on the boundary that governs translation.
