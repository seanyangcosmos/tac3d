A recurring failure mode in scientific interpretation is treating evidence volume as evidence balance. Local coherence inside one layer can feel persuasive, but confidence in translation is layer‑dependent and sequence‑gated.

A topology‑aware approach keeps the order intact: first establish structure at the core semantic layer, then arbitrate cross‑evidence consistency at the decision layer, and only then translate into narrative implications at the overlay layer. Reversing this order (starting from interpretation) inflates confidence without repairing missing structure.

Evidence asymmetry is the norm: mechanistic signals can be strong while genetic anchors are tentative, or early clinical hints can arrive before pathway clarity. In each case, partial‑stack convergence is not validation. Support can accumulate locally while the transfer function to the next layer remains gated.

Uncertainty propagates differently across layers. Mis-specified mechanisms bleed uncertainty into genetics; weak genetic reinforcement caps clinical priors; and clinical noise cannot backfill upstream gaps. Treat transfer as earned—explicitly closed by cross‑layer checks—rather than assumed by momentum.

Decision implication: before advancing, ask three structure‑first questions—(1) Which layer actually carries the signal? (2) What gate must close to transfer confidence upstream or downstream? (3) Where does unresolved uncertainty enter the stack? Maintaining this discipline preserves stack integrity and prevents narrative from outpacing structure.
