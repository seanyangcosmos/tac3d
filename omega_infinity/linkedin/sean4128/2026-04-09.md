Biomedical reasoning becomes difficult when evidence does not fail cleanly.

A translational gap is one example. Mechanistic structure may look coherent, genetic signal may appear directionally supportive, and yet clinical evidence may remain incomplete, noisy, or absent. That does not produce a simple negative conclusion. It produces a structurally unresolved one.

Cross-layer uncertainty matters because each layer answers a different question. Mechanistic evidence addresses plausibility. Genetic evidence can strengthen target-level confidence. Clinical evidence tests whether the proposed effect closes at the human outcome layer. When these layers do not resolve together, decision difficulty increases.

The challenge is not only missing data. It is the distribution of uncertainty across the stack. A problem may look strong from below and still remain blocked above. Another may show clinical movement while retaining unresolved mechanistic conflict. In both cases, the difficulty comes from structural mismatch rather than lack of activity.

This is why translational reasoning is rarely about counting supportive signals. The harder problem is deciding what kind of uncertainty remains, where it sits, and whether the structure is sufficient to justify movement across layers.
