Teams accumulate “interpretation debt” when they trade structural closure for a faster story. Every unmet gate spawns follow‑on work: extra subgroup plots, unplanned sensitivity cuts, expanded endpoints that make the same open question feel answered. Debt compounds until the schedule breaks.

Topology is the antidote. It asks one harsh question at each step: what is the smallest resolving endpoint that flips the current gate under the hardest skeptic—and what falsifier would make us stop? Everything else is supportive by design. It can inform, not decide.

Error surfaces rarely match across layers. A surrogate that is crisp and low‑variance mechanistically can be noisy or orthogonal clinically. A clean functional readout can still fail to move the human layer if reimbursement or workflow stay open. Calibration must precede aggregation: align to the next layer’s closure criteria before you stack more data in the current one.

Operationally this means replacing volume with order. Publish the resolver, its minimal important difference, and the falsifier up front. Tie resourcing to resolving designs, not expressive catalogs. When a resolver fails, pay the debt immediately by stopping or redesigning, not by narrating around it.

The result is less drama and more transfer. Stakeholders hear “gate closed” or “falsifier fired,” not another chapter of progress adjectives. Confidence becomes a permission you earn in sequence, not a feeling you borrow from the last good chart.
