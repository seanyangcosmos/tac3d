A common trap in translational reasoning is assuming that coherence equals readiness. A dataset can be internally consistent, a mechanism can look elegant, and a small study can read cleanly—yet the system may still be unprepared for transfer. Topology asks a different question: has the right gate actually closed?

The order matters. Start with the core semantic layer to establish structure and boundary conditions. Move to the decision layer only when the evidence objects are shaped and comparable, so arbitration is about alignment rather than persuasion. Only after that do we allow the overlay to translate structure into narrative. Reversing the order turns interpretation into momentum, not insight.

Evidence rarely arrives symmetrically. Mechanistic support can mature before genetic anchors; genetic hints can outpace clinical signal; and clinical noise can masquerade as confirmation. Partial‑stack agreement is not validation. Confidence is a layer‑local property until a transfer gate—explicitly defined and checked—moves it downstream or upstream.

Uncertainty also has direction. A fuzzy mechanism contaminates downstream priors; weak genetic reinforcement caps the ceiling on clinical claims; a single clean clinical readout cannot fix upstream mis‑specification. The discipline is to name where uncertainty enters and to prevent it from leaking across layers by default.

Decision implication: protect stack integrity by writing the gating question into the plan—what must be true, at which layer, for confidence to move? If the answer is vague, the project is still pre‑transfer. Keep building structure; postpone the narrative until the gate actually shuts.
