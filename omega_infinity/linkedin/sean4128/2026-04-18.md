A recurring failure mode in scientific interpretation is starting with narrative and retrofitting the structure after the fact. When interpretation leads, uncertainty compounds silently. The fix isn’t louder claims; it’s enforcing the order of operations.

Treat the core semantic interface as the root of truth: establish the question, the goal, and the structured evidence objects first. Only then route through a decision layer that arbitrates across mechanistic, genetic, and clinical structures. Cross-evidence evaluation is a structural act, not a rhetorical one.

Keep layer asymmetry explicit. Strong local alignment (e.g., mechanistic plausibility plus supportive genetics) does not guarantee global readiness if the clinical layer is thin or mis-specified. Partial-stack convergence is not validation; confidence propagates only when transfer gates are actually closed.

Use the overlay last, and only to translate settled structure into human-readable strategy. Interpretation should clarify the decision object, not redefine it. If the overlay begins to add “evidence,” you’ve inverted the stack and are manufacturing certainty.

Practical loop: build the structured evidence at the core, run cross-layer arbitration to expose tensions and constraints, then render the narrative for stakeholders. This preserves stack integrity, keeps uncertainty where it belongs, and makes decisions legible without pretending that unclosed layers are solved.
