Biomedical reasoning often breaks down when support is distributed unevenly across mechanistic, genetic, and clinical layers.

A translational gap is one form of that problem. Mechanistic evidence may suggest causal plausibility, genetic evidence may reinforce target relevance, and clinical evidence may still remain absent, weak, or structurally inconclusive. The result is not a clean negative. It is a decision problem shaped by incomplete transfer across layers.

Cross-layer uncertainty makes these cases difficult because the layers do not answer the same question. Mechanistic structure addresses whether an effect is plausible. Genetic structure can strengthen confidence in alignment at the target level. Clinical structure addresses whether the signal resolves at the human outcome layer. When those layers fail to close together, uncertainty does not disappear. It shifts upward into the decision boundary.

That is where structural decision difficulty emerges. A stack with lower-layer coherence can still remain non-advancing because the unresolved layer is the one that governs transfer. In other cases, human signal may appear before causal structure is settled, creating a different kind of cross-layer instability. The hard part is not counting supportive observations. It is determining whether the full evidence topology supports movement across layers without leaving the central uncertainty unresolved.
