Measure what decides: building resolving endpoints across layers

Most programs do not fail for lack of data. They fail because they collect data that cannot decide the next question. The instinct to accumulate supportive signals is strong: more histograms, more subgroups, more marginal p-values. None of that changes the topology. Confidence is local until a specific gate closes, and gates close only when a resolving endpoint moves by a predeclared amount.

The reasoning failure here is subtle. We confuse movement with decisiveness. A biomarker budges, a model loss improves, an early functional measure ticks up. These are useful inside their layer. They are not, by themselves, transferable. The next layer cares about a different thing with a different error surface. A resolving endpoint is the minimal measurement that binds the next layer’s decision. It is not the easiest metric to move, and it is not the prettiest chart. It is whatever your hardest skeptic will accept as sufficient to change the gate’s state.

Define the topology constraint this way: ordered closure, not expressive accumulation. If you do not know which endpoint closes the current gate, you do not know what “success” means. Topology makes that ignorance explicit and forces an architectural correction. You must pick an endpoint and a minimal important difference that the next layer recognizes as decisive. Only then do sub-measures, secondary signals, and exploratory features make sense. Without that anchor, every other measurement risks becoming narrative fuel rather than structural proof.

Layer interaction looks different under this discipline. In the mechanistic layer, you design to earn an effect size that causally links a perturbation to pathway behavior, complete with an off-target profile. This is not a catalog of everything that moved; it is a proof of the thing that must move to make the next claim plausible. In the clinical layer, you bind function to prior biology using endpoints and multiplicity control that a regulator will consider meaningful. In the human layer, you treat adoption as a gate with its own resolving endpoints: coverage criteria satisfied, workflow friction removed, prescriber behavior durable over a defined window.

The implications for decision making are immediate:
- Resolve, do not express. A small study that can close a gate is more valuable than a large study that can only describe it.
- Time-box uncertainty. If a gate cannot be closed inside the budget and timeline, call the no-go early and reallocate to designs that can resolve.
- Set falsifiers. A resolving endpoint without a falsifier invites motivated interpretation. Name what would make you stop.
- Validate meaning. For multi-jurisdiction programs, confirm what counts as “clinically meaningful” with the hardest regulator and adopt that standard across sites.

Uncertainty propagation is another place where programs drift. We often act as if uncertainty is a cloud that will thin if we collect enough points. Topology says some points are orthogonal to the decision and will not move the next layer no matter how many you collect. The right move is not “more data,” it is “the right data,” and often fewer of them. This is not anti-science, it is pro-decision. You are architecting an argument that can survive skepticism in order, not curating a museum of interesting effects.

Jurisdictional asymmetry deserves its own note. When one regulator rejects while another allows restricted use, that is rarely a problem of messaging style. It is a problem of closure criteria. Different agencies weight evidence and risk differently. The only robust way through is to design to the hardest gate and let everything else inherit. In practice: pre-submission dialogue to confirm minimal important differences, endpoint hierarchy that reflects that agreement, and a multiplicity plan that survives hostile reading.

Adoption, finally, is not something that happens after the science is done. It is a gate that requires evidence. Resolve it with targeted pilots sized to answer operational questions: can this be billed as intended, does it fit the care pathway without creating drag, does prescriber behavior persist beyond the novelty window, what does the safety profile look like under real-world heterogeneity. Put those outcomes on the roadmap with the same clarity you put your clinical endpoints.

A practical template for building resolving endpoints across layers:
1) Map gates and write minimal closure criteria in the order they must be earned.
2) For the current gate, publish the resolving endpoint, its minimal important difference, and the falsifier.
3) Design the smallest study that can change the gate’s state with acceptable error; commit to stopping rules.
4) Make supportive measures subordinate by design; they cannot override the resolving endpoint.
5) For multi-jurisdiction work, validate “meaningful” with the hardest skeptic and anchor on that.
6) For adoption, define operational endpoints and the evidence that will count as closure.

If this sounds unglamorous, that is the point. Topology turns momentum into finished work by forcing the definition of success to match the next decision. It does not ask for more faith or more optimism. It asks for more architecture. When teams design for closure instead of movement, they ship fewer studies and more outcomes. That is how hard problems cross the gap from demonstration to decision.
