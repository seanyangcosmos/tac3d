Many teams still mistake momentum for balance. When mechanistic, genetic, or clinical signals cluster inside a single layer, it feels like global readiness. But evidence volume within a layer is not evidence balance across the stack, and it does not, by itself, unlock transfer to the next layer.

The constraint is structural: confidence is local and gated. Each layer has a distinct error surface and observability window. Support can meaningfully reshape interpretation geometry inside a layer while leaving the gate above it unchanged. Advancement requires ordered closure, not rhetorical elevation.

Asymmetry is the rule, not the exception. Mechanistic clarity without genetic reinforcement leaves external validity open. Genetic association without mechanism risks spurious linkage. Clinical signal without upstream coherence invites overfitting. Uncertainty only propagates upward when the current layer’s constraints are satisfied.

Decision framing should center on the next unopened gate. Ask: what evidence closes the present layer, what would falsify this step, and what is the minimal design that changes the gate’s state? Treat interpretation as downstream of structure; resist promoting local alignment into global conclusions until closure is earned.

In practice: keep a living map of layer status, define minimal closure criteria per layer, separate accumulation from transfer, and report residual translation risk explicitly. Partial‑stack convergence guides resourcing and sequencing; it is not validation. Topology protects decision integrity by preserving the order in which confidence is allowed to propagate.
