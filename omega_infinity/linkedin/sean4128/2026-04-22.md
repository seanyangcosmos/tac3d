One of the easiest mistakes in cross‑jurisdiction work is assuming that regulatory alignment is a communication problem. It is usually a topology problem. The same dossier can be coherent inside one evidence layer and still fail to close the gate in another agency’s ordering.

Topology treats confidence as local and transfer as earned. Jurisdictional asymmetry is not noise. it encodes different closure criteria and error tolerances. A dataset that moves interpretation geometry in one context might not bind the clinical or social outcome that another regulator requires. When you mistake tone for structure, you try to fix misalignment with narrative instead of measurement.

Asymmetry also runs forward into adoption. Even when the technical layers close, the human layer can keep the gate open. Care pathways, incentives, and reimbursement are not downstream niceties. They are part of the ordered stack. Pushing interpretation ahead of those gates just moves the bottleneck to launch.

Decision discipline here is simple. Map the gates per jurisdiction. Define the minimal closure criteria in the order they must be earned. Design the smallest resolving study for the hardest skeptic, then let every other conversation inherit from that structure. When alignment happens, it looks like multiple layers changing state in order, not like a better story about the same open gate.
