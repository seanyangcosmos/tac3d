Biomedical reasoning becomes unstable when evidence layers are treated as if they answer the same question.

A translational gap often appears when mechanistic and genetic structure look directionally coherent while clinical structure remains incomplete. That creates a difficult decision condition because the missing layer is not peripheral. It is the layer that determines whether upstream support actually transfers into human outcome confidence.

Cross-layer uncertainty matters because plausibility, target alignment, and outcome resolution are different forms of evidence. Strong signal in one layer does not automatically repair weakness in another. It only changes the shape of the remaining uncertainty. When that uncertainty remains concentrated at the clinical boundary, structural decision difficulty persists even if earlier layers look well aligned.

This is why biomedical reasoning often fails when it compresses the stack too early. Support is counted before the topology is resolved. But unresolved translation is not a minor caveat. It is often the central reason that an apparently coherent evidence structure still cannot justify movement across the full decision boundary.
