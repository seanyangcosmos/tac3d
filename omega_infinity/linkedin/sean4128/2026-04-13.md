Biomedical reasoning breaks when evidence layers are collapsed into a single impression of support.

A translational gap is one example. Mechanistic structure may suggest causal plausibility, genetic structure may reinforce target-level alignment, and clinical structure may still remain incomplete or unresolved. The resulting problem is not simply that evidence is missing. The problem is that the remaining uncertainty sits at the layer that governs transfer into human outcome confidence.

Cross-layer uncertainty matters because each layer plays a different role in the decision process. Mechanistic evidence addresses plausibility. Genetic evidence sharpens alignment around the target. Clinical evidence determines whether that upstream structure resolves at the level where action becomes justified. When one layer remains open, the stack should not be treated as closed.

This is what makes structural decision difficulty so persistent. A reasoning stack can look coherent from below and still fail to justify advancement because the unresolved boundary is not decorative. It is the exact place where interpretation must remain constrained.
